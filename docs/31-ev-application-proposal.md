## Proposal

### About me

I was born and raised in Kazakhstan and have spent ten-plus years in technology, starting as a C++ engineer and moving into product management for B2B/B2G software and consumer apps. I am a Senior Product Manager at a major mobility tech company, one of the largest tech exports out of Central Asia. In 2024 I owned the activation surface for new passengers and shipped the highest single-quarter activation improvement in company history — four percentage points — while contributing to a thirty-percent increase in intercity GMV. I left a major mobility tech company in 2025 to work on Horecom full-time.

The most useful thing I have done was not at a major mobility tech company. In 2019 I co-founded (redacted), a fresh-grocery delivery operation in Kazakhstan. For the first three months we wrote no code. My co-founder and I took weekend trips to wholesale markets, hand-fulfilled orders for whoever WhatsApped us, and routed deliveries in Excel. We grew to 17,000 deliveries to 5,000 families across four cities. Then we made the obvious mistake: when the delivery market overheated post-pandemic, we chased topline growth instead of fixing the operational gaps the growth had exposed. The team disengaged. The project closed four months after the team's energy left it. This taught me — at real cost — that operational rigor compounds faster than strategic cleverness, and that no growth team can outrun a broken supply chain.

I am married to co-founder. For five years she ran (redacted), a wholesale pastry-supply network in Astana, opening locations under franchise and building the supplier relationships and operational discipline most procurement startups try to learn from a deck. In 2025 the franchise holder bought her business; she retained her audience — 76,000 Instagram followers built around supplying small bakeries — and the operational knowledge of an entire customer segment. Together we launched Horecom on top of that base.

### One mainstream view I absolutely agree with

I agree that **boring operational rigor compounds faster than strategic cleverness, and that this is the most underused source of advantage in early-stage businesses.** This is consensus on Munger, Buffett, and Stripe Press; almost no founder I know actually behaves as if they believe it. They reach for clever pricing models, growth loops, or category re-naming long before they have a reliable substitution policy, a working returns flow, or an honest unit-economic baseline.

(redacted) taught me this expensively. The cleverness was real — we had a working delivery operation, smart Excel, decent unit economics in the early stages — but the boring part (operational quality controls, on-time delivery rate, customer-NPS process) was treated as a thing we would fix later. We never reached later. Horecom is being built the other way around: the boring foundations are designed before the headline features. Our technical specification closes thirteen named operational gaps that two independent expert reviews missed, before we have written a line of platform code.

### The idea

Central Asia has roughly one hundred million people, a fast-growing HoReCa segment, and effectively no software-enabled B2B procurement. Restaurants and bakeries order ingredients from suppliers through WhatsApp screenshots and Excel ledgers. There is no Choco, no Pepper, no Rekki for this region. Pepper raised $50M in February 2026 in the US for exactly this category; nobody is building the equivalent here.

Horecom is the operating beginning of that platform. We have run for fourteen months on a Tilda MVP that does not even display prices on its product cards. We have generated $620,000 in gross sales (~$43k per month), serve 50+ active B2B accounts, run 10+ small pastry shops on recurring subscription delivery, hold 50 supplier relationships, and have ~5,000 unique WhatsApp contacts. Our Instagram has 76,000 followers, our Threads 9,900. Web conversion from session to lead sits at 3.05%; 90% of traffic is mobile. Average order value is approximately $88.

What is novel about Horecom is not the catalog, but the architecture on top of it. We design for three customer segments with three different value modes on one shared catalog and physical infrastructure:

1. **Fast wholesale ordering** for large HoReCa businesses — restaurants and cafes that want fast cart-building and reorder shortcuts;
2. **Predictive subscription replenishment** for small bakeries without warehouse space — calculating when each ingredient will run out and delivering proactively;
3. **Group buying** for self-employed home bakers who cannot meet wholesale minimums alone — pooling demand to access wholesale prices without holding wholesale inventory.

No regional competitor offers all three. Most consumer marketplaces in the region serve only B2C delivery (Wolt, Glovo, Yandex). Local B2B suppliers operate over WhatsApp. We have already validated demand for all three modes manually: segments one and two are paying customers today, segment three has run twice through ad-hoc WhatsApp groups.

The grant does not fund the search for product-market fit. We have it. The grant funds the replacement of the Tilda MVP — which cannot scale beyond Astana or differentiate between customer segments — with a real platform: a web application, predictive replenishment engine, group buying workspace, WhatsApp-native ordering, and a substitution UX that does not silently swap out-of-stock items. The technical specification is complete and ready for development.

Budget: **$20,000**, allocated as follows.

- **$12,000 — founder operating runway for six months.** Maintains current operations and product velocity while the platform is built, so we do not have to advise/consult on the side to cover personal costs.
- **$5,000 — part-time developer hire for three months.** Enables parallel work on the group buying module so it ships in V1 rather than V1.5.
- **$3,000 — Almaty pilot acquisition.** Field marketing and onboarding for the first 20 accounts in our second city.

What success looks like in six months: 200+ active accounts (4x current), measured D30 subscription retention above 70%, first cohort live in Almaty, AI-discoverability layer deployed (`/llms.txt`, Schema.org, Bing Webmaster registration) so HoReCa operators asking ChatGPT "where to buy Barry Callebaut wholesale in Astana" can find us.

The counterfactual is honest: we will build this anyway, because the operation is profitable. The grant compresses our timeline from roughly twelve months to roughly six, and removes the temptation to take capital from a local strategic investor whose conditions would push us toward less ambitious geography. EV's specific value is not the dollar amount — it is the speed of the decision, the network of grantees who have thought about building in similar contexts, and the external signal that infrastructure software for Central Asia is fundable. Local Kazakhstani capital is largely allocated to B2C consumer plays, mining, and agritech; an EV grant would be the first international valuation anchor available to us.

The long version of "if everything works": Horecom becomes the default procurement infrastructure for ~50,000 HoReCa businesses across five Central Asian countries, the regional analogue to Choco and Rekki. Take rate on GMV plus SaaS subscription for predictive replenishment plus factoring for HoReCa. Data is the long-term moat; we will know exactly what each of those businesses orders, when, and at what price.

Two co-founders, both in Astana. Working business with cashflow. Ten years in tech. Three customer segments validated. A platform spec ready to implement. We are not looking for cash to find the idea — we have it. We are looking for the runway to deploy it.
